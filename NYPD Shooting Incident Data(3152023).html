PART 5 - Bias and Criticisms

The dataset used in this study comprises shooting incidents reported and recorded by the NYC Police Department, but the accuracy of these reports is subject to various limitations. For instance, some incidents may not be reported at all, leading to an underestimation of the actual number of shooting incidents. Boroughs with higher reported incidents may not necessarily have had more shootings, as there may have been a failure to report due to various reasons, such as reluctance to report to the police, fear of retaliation, or perceived police bias. Factors affecting reporting, such as police budget, personnel, and locale availability, can be quantified, while others, such as individual and community concerns and beliefs, are more difficult to measure.

The data collection process may also be biased and discriminatory, such as the collection of perpetrator race, which suggests a suspected difference among races and may contribute to disproportionate numbers of same-race or different-race incidents. Therefore, caution should be exercised when interpreting the results and making recommendations based on seemingly objective data. For example, increasing police presence in areas with higher incidence may not necessarily address the underlying reasons for increased numbers of incidents or reduce the number of incidents. These challenges highlight the difficulties in making subjective decisions and recommendations based on incomplete or biased data that may not fully capture the complexity of the real world.
